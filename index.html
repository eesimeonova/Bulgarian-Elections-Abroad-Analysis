Fewer Bulgarians Voted in Turkey After Polling Stations Abroad Were Capped

After the new limit was introduced, 17,578 fewer people voted in Turkey than in the previous parliamentary election

By Elitsa Simeonova

Turkey was the only country where the number of voters declined after the Bulgarian parliament capped the number of polling stations abroad, an analysis of election data shows. After the new limit was introduced, 17,578 fewer people voted in Turkey than in the previous parliamentary election.

What Changed

Bulgaria has held eight parliamentary elections since 2021. The most recent took place on April 19, 2026. Less than two months earlier, the Bulgarian parliament changed the rules for opening polling stations abroad.

The new rules limit the number of polling stations that can be opened in countries outside the European Union to 20, in addition to those located at embassies. Bulgarian law does not allow citizens to vote by post or electronically, leaving Bulgarians abroad with no option but to vote in person.

Election experts warned that limiting the number of polling stations could reduce the number of people voting abroad.

The change was proposed by the pro-Russian nationalist Revival party and supported by the then-ruling coalition. Supporters said it was intended to limit voting in Turkey, where they claimed that "voting is widespread and illegal."

Turkey is home to a large community of Bulgarian voters as a result of the former communist regime's forced assimilation campaign against Bulgaria's Turkish minority.

In the final year of communist rule, the authorities under dictator Todor Zhivkov deported about 320,000 people—an estimated one-third of the country's Turkish population—to Turkey. Those deported and their descendants are entitled to Bulgarian citizenship and, therefore, have the right to vote in Bulgarian elections.

Critics argued that the change would "punish" Bulgarian citizens abroad because of the way they vote. Opposition parties said it violated the constitutional right of Bulgarians living abroad to vote.

Polling Stations Were Cut in Three Countries

This analysis compares the October 2024 and April 2026 parliamentary elections using polling station and voting data published by Bulgaria's Central Election Commission (CEC). The number of voters is based on the signatures in polling station voter lists published by the CEC. Because Bulgaria does not maintain a register of eligible voters living abroad, voter turnout cannot be calculated.

The number of polling stations was reduced in three countries:

  • Turkey
  • The United Kingdom
  • The United States

In Turkey, the number of polling stations fell from 168 in October 2024 to 27 in April 2026. In the United Kingdom, it fell from 112 to 28, while in the United States it declined from 53 to 24.

The reduction left some Bulgarian communities in these countries without a nearby polling station. There were reports of voters travelling hundreds of kilometres and waiting in line for hours to vote.

The Number of Voters Increased Overall, but Declined in Turkey

Overall, more people voted abroad in the April 2026 parliamentary election than in October 2024. According to CEC data, 196,281 people voted abroad in April 2026, compared with 151,429 in October 2024.

In both the United Kingdom and the United States, more people voted in April 2026 despite the significantly lower number of polling stations.

Turkey, however, saw 17,578 fewer people vote than in October 2024. A total of 30,229 people voted in April 2026, compared with 47,807 in the previous parliamentary election.

Other factors may also have contributed to the decline, including a split within the party that has traditionally received the most support from Bulgarian voters in Turkey.

However, the decline in the number of people voting in Turkey is consistent with election experts' warnings that limiting the number of polling stations abroad could discourage voting.
